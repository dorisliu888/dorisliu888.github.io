Q: How does your participant’s linguistic and cultural background inform their beliefs about the connection between language, culture, and education?
A: Doris comes from a background of very traditional Taiwanese culture. Her grandparents, parents, cousins and most of her family grew up in Taiwan and were exposed to its ideologies and cultural values ever since they were young. Doris on the other hand, experienced a different side of Taiwanese Culture, one that was integrated, and sometimes conflicting, with American societal values. Ever since she was young, Doris was put in a very different society than her parents, learned the foreign language rapidly, and was constantly exposed to battling ideologies of individualism, self-independence, value of education, and meritocracy. Overall, these experiences have lead her to believe in certain ideologies that explain the connection between language, culture and education. For example, from her Taiwanese Heritage and culture, she adopted a high value of education, and feels that it is almost a cultural requirement to do well in school. Not doing well in school can disconnect you from your family and lead to negative impressions on your whole family by strangers. To highlight this, she says a "C" is basically failing in her culture. Another example is her sense of self-independence, which she feels is different from most American students. She often feels restrained by her parents and unable to truly express who she is. Finally, as a result of being exposed to hybrid Taiwanese-American culture and immigrant work ideology, she believes strongly in the American Dream and in the merit of hard work.
Beliefs About Language, Culture, Education

Q: How does your participant’s linguistic and cultural background shape their identity, sense of self, status, etc.?
A: Doris identifies strongly as a Taiwanese American, Proud Female, and belonging to the professional group of societal urban planners and designers. To show her dedication to these titles, she frequently expresses herself through visual artworks and design pieces, values her Taiwanese family connections, and holds strong views about female enpowerment in modern society. The reason she identifies as Taiwanese American rather than strictly Taiwanese has to do with the type of exposure to each culture she has experienced throughout her life. In general, due to conflicting American and Taiwanese ideologies, she is not able to fully accept either one. There are aspects of both cultures she firmly believes to be true and holds dear. This mix of cultural ideologies forms her sense of identity, self and status. In a way, she feels like a bridge between the two worlds since she can speak both Mandarin and English, and respects both cultures equally.
Identity, Sense of Self, Status

Q: What advice would your participant give future teachers to provide equitable opportunities to learn for students from all backgrounds?
A: There are multiple complaints Doris has about the current education system in America, and a couple of suggestions to fix these. Mostly, she has expressed complaints of having both quarter systems AND semester systems since this is very inconvenient to students who have siblings and family members in other schools with other systems. Also opportunity-wise, there is greater career opportunity for students who start early. She has grievances about the huge student-to-teacher ratio where people can’t really learn properly. Most of all, she doesn't like how the system doesn't hire teachers based on skill, but rather based on how long they have been there, seniority, how familiar they are with the school and how many connections they have. This applies to jobs in other industries, clubs, and other institutions too. People shouldn’t be rewarded for having connections to powerful people or influential people, but rather they should be rewarded for having skill, and earning that reward through hard work and dedication. If there is one thing she would want to change about the education system, it is to make sure that the people the system hire to teach get vetted correctly and make sure they are actually credible to teach. No matter how motivated the students are, their success really depends on how well the teacher teaches the class material. This would lead to more equitable opportunities for teachers of diverse language backgrounds to teach and to provide a more diverse curriculum to accomodate students of all cultures.
